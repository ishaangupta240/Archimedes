What is Burning Mirrors ?

According to historical accounts, during the Siege of Syracuse in 212 BC, when the Roman fleet was attacking the city, Archimedes is said to have devised a system of large mirrors or polished bronze shields that could focus sunlight onto the Roman ships, causing them to catch fire. This legendary event is mentioned by ancient writers like the Roman author Lucian and the Greek historian Polybius, but the accounts can vary in details.

A Closer Look at the Basics of the Archimedes Screw

The basic idea was that Archimedes used the mirrors to concentrate sunlight onto a single point, effectively creating an intense beam of heat that could ignite flammable materials on the Roman ships. Some versions of the story describe a series of mirrors working together to focus the sunlight, while others suggest the use of large, curved mirrors or even parabolic mirrors.

While the concept of using mirrors to focus sunlight and generate heat is theoretically possible (similar principles are used in solar concentrators today), there are doubts about the practicality of this feat as described in the historical accounts. Some modern experiments have attempted to replicate the Archimedes heat ray, and while they have shown that it's possible to focus sunlight with mirrors, achieving the level of concentration required to set ships on fire would have been extremely challenging, if not impossible.

Application of Burning Mirror

The first use of a burning mirror by Archimedes was for the purpose of war, with the burning glass being as a weapon in 212 BC, when Syracuse was besieged by Marcus Claudius Marcellus. The Roman fleet was supposedly incinerated, though eventually the city was taken and Archimedes was slain.
